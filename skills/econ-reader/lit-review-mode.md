# MODE 2: REVIEW

## Overview
Takes paper snapshot files (generated by Read Mode or manually created) and synthesizes them into a draft literature review. Supports two output types:
- **Embedded review**: A 1–3 paragraph contribution section for an empirical paper introduction (Cochrane/Bellemare convention)
- **Standalone survey**: A full JEL/JES-style literature review article (multi-section, thematic, 3,000–15,000 words)

## Workflow

### Step R0: Input and Scope
1. Identify all paper snapshot `.md` files in the specified directory or file list
2. Parse YAML frontmatter from each file to extract: citekey, methodology, tags, relevance, year
3. Ask the user (or infer from context):
   - **Review type**: embedded (for a paper intro) or standalone (survey article)
   - **Central question**: What is YOUR paper's research question? (for embedded) or What topic are you surveying? (for standalone)
   - **Your contribution**: What is the specific thing your paper does differently? (for embedded only)

### Step R1: Literature Landscape Analysis
From all parsed snapshots, automatically generate:

**Coverage matrix** — a synthesis table:
| Citekey | Year | Method | Setting | Key Finding | Gap Relative to Your Work |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

**Cluster detection** — group papers by:
1. **By identification strategy**: DiD papers, IV papers, RCT papers, structural papers, etc.
2. **By mechanism**: Which causal channel each paper tests
3. **By finding**: Positive / negative / null / heterogeneous results
4. **By setting**: Geography, population, time period

Select the organizing principle that best highlights YOUR contribution:
- If your contribution is a better identification strategy → organize by method
- If your contribution is a new mechanism → organize by theory
- If your contribution resolves conflicting findings → organize by finding
- If your contribution is a new setting/population → organize by setting

### Step R2: Draft Generation

#### For Embedded Review (empirical paper intro)

Follow the Head/Bellemare/Cochrane formula:

**Contribution paragraph structure** (1–3 paragraphs, targeting 5–7 citations):

```
This paper contributes to [X] strands of literature.

First, we contribute to the literature on [STRAND 1: broadest].
[TOPIC SENTENCE naming the theme]. [2–4 citations with brief
characterization of findings/methods, organized by sub-theme].
[TRANSITION: state what remains unknown or unresolved].

Second, we relate to work on [STRAND 2: more specific].
[Same structure]. We extend this work by [SPECIFIC MARGINAL
CONTRIBUTION — better data, stronger identification, new
mechanism, new population].

[Optional third strand, same structure.]

Relative to the most closely related paper, [AUTHOR (YEAR)],
we differ in [PRECISE DIMENSION OF DIFFERENTIATION].
```

**Rules for embedded review**:
- Maximum 3 strands (4 is upper bound; beyond that the paper appears unfocused)
- 5–7 total citations (Bellemare benchmark), max ~12 for complex contributions
- Focus on the 2–3 closest papers (Cochrane benchmark) — give them the most space
- Funnel structure: broadest strand → narrowest → single closest paper
- Every citation must serve the argument that YOUR contribution matters
- Be generous: "We build on X" not "X failed to do Y"
- State contributions as additive, not adversarial
- Use author-date citations: "Card and Krueger (1994) find..." or "(Card and Krueger 1994)"

#### For Standalone Survey (JEL/JES style)

Follow JEL editorial standards: synthesis with a point of view, selective coverage, clear statement of open questions.

**Structure template**:

```markdown
# [Survey Title]

## 1. Introduction
- Why this topic matters now
- Scope and boundaries of the review
- Preview of organizing framework
- Roadmap of sections

## 2. [Thematic Section 1: e.g., "Theoretical Foundations"]
- Core models and predictions
- Key assumptions and their implications
- Where theory is settled vs. contested

## 3. [Thematic Section 2: e.g., "Identification Strategies"]
- Organize by method (DiD, IV, RD, RCT, structural)
- For each: seminal paper, key findings, strengths/limitations
- Literature comparison table

## 4. [Thematic Section 3: e.g., "Empirical Evidence"]
- Organize by finding or by sub-question
- Highlight points of consensus and disagreement
- Debate map: what drives conflicting results?

## 5. [Thematic Section 4: e.g., "Mechanisms and Heterogeneity"]
- Causal channels proposed and tested
- Heterogeneous effects across populations/settings
- What mediators/moderators have been examined?

## 6. Open Questions and Future Directions
- Boundary gaps (unexplored settings/populations)
- Mechanism gaps (under-tested channels)
- Measurement gaps (better proxies needed)
- Design gaps (stronger identification possible)
- Concrete research agenda (3–5 specific proposals)

## 7. Conclusion
- State of the literature in 3–5 sentences
- Most important unresolved tension
- What the next generation of research should prioritize
```

**Rules for standalone survey**:
- Thematic organization, NEVER paper-by-paper
- Each paragraph covers one THEME, citing multiple papers
- End each thematic section with an assessment of where evidence stands
- Include at least one literature comparison table per major section
- It is NOT necessary to cite every paper — selectivity guided by expertise is a feature (JEL standard)
- State your point of view but fairly represent opposing views
- 3,000–15,000 words depending on scope (JES benchmark: 10,000–15,000)

### Step R3: Literature Comparison Table Generation

For each major section, generate a comparison table:

```markdown
| Author(s) | Year | Data/Setting | Identification | DV | Key Finding | N |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Card & Krueger | 1994 | NJ/PA fast food | DiD | Employment | No decline after MW increase | 410 |
| Neumark & Wascher | 2000 | BLS payroll data | DiD | Employment | Significant decline | ~230 |
```

### Step R4: Debate Map Generation

When papers disagree, generate a structured debate map:

```markdown
### Debate: [Topic]

**Position A: [Finding]**
- Supported by: [Author1 (Year), Author2 (Year)]
- Mechanism: [Why they find this]
- Identification: [Design used]

**Position B: [Opposing finding]**
- Supported by: [Author3 (Year), Author4 (Year)]
- Mechanism: [Why they find differently]
- Identification: [Design used]

**Sources of disagreement:**
- [ ] Different data sources
- [ ] Different identification strategies
- [ ] Different populations/time periods
- [ ] Different variable definitions
- [ ] Different functional form assumptions

**Resolution (if any):** [What would settle this — or what your paper does]
```

### Step R5: Quality Checks

Before saving, verify the draft against economics conventions:

**Anti-patterns to flag and fix:**
- [ ] **Telephone directory**: Any paragraph discussing only one paper → restructure around themes
- [ ] **Buried contribution**: Contribution statement appears after paragraph 3 → move earlier
- [ ] **Disconnected review**: Any paragraph that doesn't set up YOUR contribution → cut or reframe
- [ ] **Over-comprehensiveness**: More than ~12 citations in embedded review → trim to closest work
- [ ] **Missing debate**: Papers with conflicting findings listed without explaining WHY they disagree
- [ ] **Adversarial framing**: "X failed to..." or "X incorrectly..." → rewrite as additive ("We build on X by...")
- [ ] **Plot summary syndrome**: Describing what papers DO rather than what they FIND or MEAN → rewrite as synthesis
- [ ] **Asterisk worship**: Characterizing findings only by significance level without economic magnitude → add effect sizes

# IMPORTANT METHODOLOGY NOTES

## For Review Mode
- NEVER organize paper-by-paper — always by theme
- Embedded reviews: 5–7 citations, 1–3 paragraphs, 2–3 strands (Bellemare/Cochrane)
- Standalone surveys: thematic sections, literature tables, debate maps, open questions
- Every citation must serve the argument that the contribution matters
- Be generous and additive, not adversarial
- Use author-date citations exclusively (never footnotes for references)
- Working papers are fully acceptable to cite (standard economics convention)
- JEL codes help scope searches but supplement with keyword + citation chasing
- Selectivity is a feature — the JEL standard: "not necessary to cite every paper"
- Each paragraph = one theme, multiple papers, ending with assessment of evidence state
