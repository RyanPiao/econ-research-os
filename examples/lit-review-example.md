## Contribution Section Draft

This paper contributes to two strands of literature.

First, we contribute to the large literature studying the employment effects of minimum wage increases. The seminal work of Card and Krueger (1994) challenged the prevailing consensus by finding no employment losses in New Jersey fast-food restaurants following a state minimum wage increase, using a difference-in-differences design with phone survey data. Neumark and Wascher (2000) contested this finding using payroll records and argued that employment effects were in fact negative, reigniting a debate that has persisted for three decades. More recent work has exploited richer variation and more credible designs. Dube, Lester, and Reich (2010) compare employment in contiguous county pairs straddling state borders, finding minimal disemployment effects and arguing that earlier negative estimates were driven by spatial heterogeneity rather than the minimum wage itself. Cengiz, Dube, Lindner, and Zipperer (2019) pool all state and federal minimum wage increases over two decades using a bunching estimator and find that while the number of jobs paying below the new minimum falls sharply, there is no corresponding decrease in total employment — the missing jobs reappear just above the new minimum. Collectively, these papers suggest that moderate minimum wage increases have limited disemployment effects, but the evidence on large increases — particularly the recent wave of city-level policies raising wages to $15 or above — remains thin. We extend this strand by providing the first event-study estimates of employment effects for minimum wage increases exceeding 40% of the prevailing median wage, using universe-of-employers administrative data that avoids the measurement concerns raised by Neumark and Wascher.

Second, we relate to the growing literature on monopsony and employer market power in low-wage labor markets. Manning (2003) developed the modern dynamic monopsony framework, showing that even in markets with many employers, search frictions can generate upward-sloping firm-level labor supply curves that enable wage-setting power. Azar, Marinescu, and Steinbaum (2020) provide direct empirical evidence of labor market concentration using vacancy data, finding that more concentrated labor markets exhibit lower posted wages. Under monopsony, a binding minimum wage can increase both wages and employment up to a point — which would rationalize the Card and Krueger findings without invoking measurement error. We contribute to this strand by testing a direct prediction of the monopsony model: if the minimum wage increase pushes wages past the monopsony optimum, employment effects should turn negative. Our setting — with minimum wage increases large enough to potentially exceed the monopsony wage — provides a novel test of this prediction.

Relative to the most closely related paper, Cengiz et al. (2019), we differ in three dimensions. First, our data are administrative payroll records with hours, allowing us to study intensive-margin responses (hours per worker) that the bunching estimator cannot capture. Second, we focus specifically on large city-level increases rather than pooling all state-level changes, which allows us to test whether the Cengiz et al. finding of minimal disemployment extends to the magnitudes now being implemented in major US cities. Third, our event-study design traces out the dynamic path of adjustment over 8 quarters, whereas the bunching approach estimates a pooled medium-run effect.
