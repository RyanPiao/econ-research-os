# /lit-review — Economics Literature Review Drafting

## Two modes

**Embedded review** (`--type embedded`): 1–3 paragraph contribution section for an empirical paper introduction. Cochrane/Bellemare convention.

**Standalone survey** (`--type standalone`): Full JEL/JES-style literature review with thematic sections, comparison tables, and open questions. 3,000–15,000 words.

## Inputs required
- Paper snapshots from `./literature/` (reads YAML frontmatter for metadata)
- User's research question (for embedded: what is YOUR paper about?)
- User's contribution (for embedded: what does YOUR paper do differently?)
- Topic scope (for standalone: what area are you surveying?)

## Embedded review procedure

### Step 1: Identify the closest papers
1. Scan all paper snapshots tagged with the relevant topic
2. Read the "Contribution Map" section from each snapshot (generated by /read-paper)
3. Rank by relevance to the user's contribution: closest methodology → closest question → closest setting
4. Select the 5–7 most relevant (Bellemare benchmark); identify the 2–3 closest (Cochrane benchmark)

### Step 2: Identify strands
Group the selected papers into 2–3 strands (4 max). The organizing principle follows from the contribution:
- If your contribution is a **better identification strategy** → organize strands by method
- If your contribution is a **new mechanism** → organize by theoretical channel
- If your contribution **resolves conflicting findings** → organize by finding (positive/negative/null)
- If your contribution is a **new setting** → organize by population/geography/time

### Step 3: Draft contribution paragraphs

**Template:**
```markdown
This paper contributes to [X] strands of literature.

First, we contribute to the literature on [STRAND 1: broadest].
[TOPIC SENTENCE: what this strand studies]. [CITATION 1] [finds/shows/estimates]
[brief result]. [CITATION 2] [extends/challenges this by] [brief result].
[CITATION 3] [provides evidence that] [brief result]. [TRANSITION: what
remains unknown or unresolved in this strand].

Second, we relate to work on [STRAND 2: more specific].
[Same structure: topic sentence → 2–4 citations with characterization →
what's missing]. We extend this work by [SPECIFIC MARGINAL CONTRIBUTION].

Relative to the most closely related paper, [@ClosestAuthorYear],
we differ in [PRECISE DIMENSION: better data / stronger identification /
new mechanism / new population / longer time period].
```

**Rules:**
- Funnel structure: broadest strand → narrowest → single closest paper
- 5–7 total citations (max ~12 for complex contributions)
- Every citation serves the argument that YOUR contribution matters
- Be generous: "We build on X" not "X failed to do Y"
- Author-date citations: "Card and Krueger (1994) find..." or "(Card and Krueger 1994)"
- No separate "Literature Review" section heading — weave into introduction
- Each paragraph = one strand, multiple papers, ending with the gap you fill

### Step 4: Quality check
- [ ] No paper-by-paper organization (each paragraph covers one THEME)
- [ ] Contribution statement appears in first 3 paragraphs, not buried
- [ ] Every citation sets up YOUR paper's contribution
- [ ] Generous, additive framing (not adversarial)
- [ ] Closest comparison paper gets the most space and the most precise differentiation

### Step 5: Save
`./projects/[project]/drafts/contribution-section-draft.md`

## Standalone survey procedure

### Step 1: Scope and organize
1. Define the survey's boundaries: topic, time period, methods, populations
2. Read all relevant paper snapshots
3. Cluster papers by theme (not by author or chronology)
4. Select organizing framework: by method, by finding, by mechanism, or by sub-question

### Step 2: Draft using JEL standards

**Structure:**
```markdown
# [Survey Title]

## 1. Introduction
[Why this topic matters now. Scope and boundaries. Preview of framework. Roadmap.]

## 2. [Thematic Section 1]
[Prose organized by theme. Each paragraph covers one sub-theme citing multiple papers.
End each section with assessment of where evidence stands.]

### Literature comparison table
| Author(s) | Year | Data/Setting | Method | DV | Key Finding | N |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

## 3. [Thematic Section 2]
[Same structure]

## 4. [Thematic Section 3]
[Same structure]

## 5. Open Questions and Future Directions
[Boundary gaps / mechanism gaps / measurement gaps / design gaps.
3–5 specific research agenda proposals.]

## 6. Conclusion
[State of literature in 3–5 sentences. Most important unresolved tension.]
```

**Rules (from JEL editorial standards):**
- Synthesis is "the crucial ingredient" — not a catalogue of papers
- Have a point of view (acknowledge it) but fairly represent opposing views
- It is NOT necessary to cite every paper — selectivity guided by expertise is a feature
- Clear statement of important unanswered questions
- At least one literature comparison table per major section
- Each paragraph: topic sentence (the theme) → multiple papers → assessment of evidence
- 10,000–15,000 words for full survey (JES benchmark)

### Step 3: Generate comparison tables
For each major section, produce:

```markdown
| Author(s) | Year | Data/Setting | Identification | DV | Key Finding | N |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
```

Columns vary by what matters for this strand. Always include: method/identification and key finding.

### Step 4: Save
- Survey draft: `./projects/[project]/drafts/survey-draft.md`
- Comparison tables: `./projects/[project]/drafts/literature-tables.md`
